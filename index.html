Immigration

ICE is transferring New Yorkers to facilities thousands of miles away at record speed

Even before their immigration cases are reviewed by a judge, detainees are frequently moved far away from their families and lawyers within a week — and can lose access to critical rights in the process.

By Celia Bernhardt May 23, 2026

Ahmad had no reason to suspect the person knocking on his door in Nassau County several months ago was an ICE agent, so he didn’t bring his phone or wallet with him when he opened the door. An Afghan refugee residing in New York on a Special Immigrant Visa, he had lived with his wife, a US citizen, for years. He had a pending application for an adjustment of status based on their marriage.

Still, he was arrested — and moved rapidly through four different detention centers, ending up thousands of miles away, imprisoned in a remote New Mexico facility, within days.

Ahmad (a pseudonym) is one of thousands of New Yorkers caught in the dragnet of an increasingly chaotic system of ICE detention transfers. Individuals with unresolved immigration cases are not only taken into detention at a far higher rate than in previous years, but are frequently moved at whiplash speed to facilities in more hostile jurisdictions halfway across the country. They are separated from their families, face difficulty communicating with their attorneys, and are often deprived of critical legal rights they would have retained in their home region.

An analysis of ICE data shows that 2,466 people detained in the area managed by ICE’s New York City field office since Trump took office were still marked as having pending or unresolved immigration cases as of the data’s release in early March. 60% of them, or 1,488, have been transferred out of the area. 344 chose voluntary departure rather than remaining in detention.

Out of all 9,222 New Yorkers detained in the second Trump presidency, 77% have at some point endured being transferred out of the Northeast entirely, compared to only 19% under Biden.

“People have been transferred over the country before, but the scale of this is unprecedented,” American Immigration Council Senior Fellow Aaron Reichlin-Melnick said. “The sheer number of people being moved through the system is totally new. And the speed at which people are moved is often quicker than ever.”

A Fundamental Shift

The 9,222 New York area arrests since Trump took office in early 2025 are a stark increase from approximately 3,480 in the same time period under Biden; New York does not have enough beds to hold these detainees. Meanwhile, a buildout of massive detention centers in the South and Southwest — and the fact that many of the facilities are required to fill a minimum quota of beds — means that people are often moved to these regions.

Texas and Louisiana host some of the largest detention hubs. They are both located in the fifth circuit, where judges have upheld a precedent-breaking ruling that ICE detainees are wholly ineligible for bond, regardless of their case status. People may be able to file a habeas corpus petition, forcing the government to defend the legal basis for detaining them, but most will not be eligible to even try until they have been in “prolonged detention” for six or more months. With rapid transfers funneling people into these facilities and little recourse to find a way out, many have chosen voluntary departure.

“One overarching goal of this administration is to use the awfulness of detention as a tool to pressure people into giving up before they ever get deported,” Reichlin-Melnick said.

64% of those detained under Trump have been removed from the country either through deportation or voluntary deportation, a 43 percentage point jump from the Biden era.

Legal Aid attorney Grace Carney Brown, who represents Ahmad and other New Yorkers in the system, said she has seen many clients choose voluntary departure rather than face indefinite detention — especially among parents who are worried their families won’t survive without them generating income for so long.

“We've been seeing these transfers to less friendly jurisdictions that make it harder to challenge these things,” Brown said. “Louisiana and Texas have more space for bigger detention facilities. But they also have harsher immigration laws. They're less friendly on habeas, less friendly on asylum. So is it coincidental? I don't think so.”

Out of 1,687 New Yorkers in ICE detention as of March 10, 622 — over one third — were being held in fifth circuit states, with a median of 104 days spent in the detention system as a whole. 234 New Yorkers detained in those states were marked as still having unresolved cases.

Whiplash

When Brown first took on Ahmad as a client in New York, he was detained at Metropolitan Detention Center in the city — he had been brought there after being briefly held at the Nassau County jail. She scheduled a call with him, but had to cancel after finding out that he had already been transferred to Delaney Hall in Newark by the time they were supposed to speak.

“I scheduled another call with Delaney Hall,” Brown said. “As soon as that happened, I get a notice that he's transferred again: this time to Otero, New Mexico.”

Had Ahmad and Brown had time to meet, they may have been able to request bond or filed a habeas corpus petition in the second circuit, which is friendlier to such requests. But because of the speed at which Ahmad was moved from one facility to another, that was impossible.

Under Biden, New Yorkers detained by ICE remained in either the first, second or third circuit (all of them located in the Northeast and relatively friendly to immigrants) for a median of 35 days before being transferred to other regions, if they were at all. Under Trump, the median duration has been slashed to only seven days.

Ahmad’s detention facility being just over the border in New Mexico rather than Texas — and his stay there being in just the right window of time in a legal back-and-forth about the legality of bond — meant that Brown was able to secure his release in March, two months into his detention.

She coordinated with immigrant rights organizations in the area to find him a place to sleep, have a meal and find a flight home. He still didn’t have his cell phone or wallet.

Back at home with his wife, Ahmad is one of the “lucky” ones among her clients, Brown said.

“Not all my clients were fortunate enough to benefit from that timing.”
